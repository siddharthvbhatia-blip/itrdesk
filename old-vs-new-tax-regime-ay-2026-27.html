AY 2026-27 regime comparison

Old tax regime or new tax regime?

The correct comparison uses the same complete income profile under both regimes. Comparing only slab rates or only Section 80C can produce a misleading answer.

Why the lower slab rate is not the complete answer

The new regime is the default regime for eligible taxpayers, but the old regime can remain relevant where the taxpayer is eligible and has substantial exemptions or deductions. The comparison should start from gross income and separately determine taxable income and tax under each regime.

Information to include in both computations

  • Salary, pension and the applicable standard deduction
  • House-property rent, municipal taxes, interest and loss treatment
  • Bank interest, dividends and other-source income
  • Short-term and long-term capital gains with the correct special rates
  • F&O, intraday, business or professional income
  • Eligible old-regime exemptions and Chapter VI-A deductions
  • Rebate, surcharge, marginal relief and health and education cess
  • TDS, TCS, advance tax and self-assessment tax for the final payable/refund position

Business and professional taxpayers need extra caution

A taxpayer with business or professional income should not assume that the regime can be changed freely every year. The applicable option form, statutory due date and switching restrictions must be checked before filing. A late or incorrect choice may not be repairable merely by changing a figure in the return.

Special-rate income can overturn a quick estimate

Share and mutual-fund gains, property gains, virtual digital assets, lottery income and other special-rate items require separate classification. Rebate treatment may not follow the same result as normal slab-rate income. Entering the entire amount as ordinary income can therefore misstate both regimes.

A reliable comparison workflow

  1. Reconcile income with Form 16, AIS, Form 26AS, bank records and broker statements.
  2. Classify normal-rate and special-rate income separately.
  3. List deductions and exemptions with supporting evidence.
  4. Compute taxable income and tax independently under each regime.
  5. Check option eligibility, due date and any business-income restrictions.
  6. Compare the final tax, not merely taxable income or slab rates.

Frequently asked questions

Is the new regime automatically better for everyone?

No. The result depends on income composition, deductions, exemptions, house-property facts, special-rate income and eligibility.

Can business/professional taxpayers switch every year?

Additional option and switching restrictions apply. The applicable rules and required form should be checked before acting.

Should capital gains be included in the comparison?

Yes. Special-rate income can materially change tax and rebate treatment.
